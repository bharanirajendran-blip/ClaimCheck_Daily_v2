"EPA’s Misleading Claim of $1.3 Trillion in Deregulatory ‘Savings’"

Source: FactCheck.org

Confidence: 90%

The EPA's claim of $1.3 trillion in deregulatory 'savings' is misleading because it only accounts for avoided costs without considering the benefits of the emissions standards, such as fuel savings and environmental benefits. The EPA's own analysis suggests that repealing these standards could result in a net cost to consumers.

Key Evidence
  • The $1.3 trillion figure includes $1.1 trillion in avoided vehicle technology costs and $200 billion in avoided electric vehicle charger costs, but ignores benefits like fuel savings and reduced pollution.
  • FactCheck.org notes that the EPA's own regulatory impact analysis shows a potential net cost of $180 billion when fuel savings are considered.
  • Experts like Jason Schwartz and Kenneth Gillingham criticize the methodology as biased and atypical, focusing only on costs without benefits.

"Most people get food’s environmental impact completely wrong, study finds"

Source: Science Daily — Top Science

Confidence: 85%

The claim that most people misunderstand the environmental impact of food is largely accurate but slightly overstated. The study by the University of Nottingham found systematic misconceptions, particularly overestimating the impact of processed foods and underestimating the impact of nuts and beef. However, the headline exaggerates by suggesting complete ignorance rather than partial understanding skewed by oversimplified heuristics.

Key Evidence
  • The study was conducted by the University of Nottingham's School of Psychology and published in the Journal of Cleaner Production.
  • Participants were asked to sort supermarket foods into environmental impact categories, revealing misconceptions such as overestimating processed foods and underestimating nuts and beef.
  • The study used life-cycle assessment as a scientific benchmark, which evaluates factors like greenhouse gas emissions, land use, and water use.
  • Participants expressed willingness to change purchasing behavior after learning the actual impacts, indicating misconceptions are correctable with better information.
